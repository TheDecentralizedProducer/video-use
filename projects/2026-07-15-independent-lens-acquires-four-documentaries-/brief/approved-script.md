### 90s

Independent Lens — PBS's flagship documentary strand — just announced four acquisitions in a single move: 'Jane Elliott Against The World,' 'Seeds,' and two more festival prize winners. Deadline called it an acquisition binge, and that framing is worth sitting with, because it tells you something specific about where the documentary market is right now. The last few years have not been kind to the doc sector. Streaming platforms that were aggressively commissioning and acquiring documentaries in 2021 and 2022 pulled back hard. Netflix, Max, Hulu — all of them reduced their doc acquisition budgets and shifted toward IP-based content that moved subscriber numbers. Documentary filmmakers who built their distribution strategy around a streaming acquisition discovered those deals were drying up faster than anyone predicted. Independent Lens runs against that trend. PBS has a mandate that doesn't respond to quarterly subscriber churn. When they decide to acquire, they acquire on editorial merit — and they're acquiring four films right now. For an independent filmmaker navigating what's been called the documentary distribution crisis, this is meaningful signal. Broadcast licensing doesn't pay what a streaming deal does — but it keeps your rights cleaner, it reaches an audience that is specifically seeking serious nonfiction, and it doesn't drop your film into an algorithmic catalog competing for the same recommendation slot as 50,000 other titles. Four acquisitions from Independent Lens this week is active evidence that a non-streaming distribution path is still open. That's more important than it sounds right now.
