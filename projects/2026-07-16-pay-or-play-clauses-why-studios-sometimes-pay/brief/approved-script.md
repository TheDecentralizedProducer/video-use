## Scripts

### reel

Did you know, Hollywood can pay you your full contracted fee for a movie you never made. It's not some loop hole greedy actors figured, it— it's a clause called pay-or-play, and understanding it changes how you read every talent deal announcement in the trades or how you draft your own. Here's the mechanic. When a studio offers a pay-or-play commitment, they're agreeing to either produce the project or pay the attached talent their full fee regardless. The studio has no obligation to make the film — but they are financially obligated to you if they don't. From the talent side, pay-or-play converts what would otherwise be a speculative development attachment into a real financial commitment. An A-list director or a bankable actor isn't going to hold months of their availability for a production that might never get greenlit. Pay-or-play is what makes that availability commitment binding on both sides. Studios accept this structure because it's the only mechanism to lock certain-level talent during development. From the studio's perspective, it's buying optionality: they control the talent's schedule and attachment for the project while retaining the right to kill it — as long as they write the check. The most publicly documented example of this playing out: is when director Tim Burton was developing Superman Lives in the late 1990s with Nicolas Cage attached to play Superman, Cage was operating under a pay-or-play deal. The project went deep into pre-production — costume design, concept development, screen tests, the whole process. It was cancelled before a single day of principal photography. Cage received his full contracted fee, reported at approximately $20 million, for a film that was never made. That payment was legally required under the terms of the deal. For producers and financiers who are modeling development budgets: pay-or-play commitments are real liabilities that have to be accounted for from the moment you execute the deal, not from the day you get the greenlight.
