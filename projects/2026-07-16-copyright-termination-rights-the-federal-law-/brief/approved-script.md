There's a federal law that lets creators take back their copyrights from studios — decades after signing them away. Almost nobody uses it in time. Here's how it works. Under the Copyright Act of 1976, creators who assigned their work can file a termination notice and legally reclaim that copyright — but the eligibility window is narrow, the process is highly technical, and missing the deadline ends the right permanently. The law was designed to give creators a second chance to negotiate from a position of leverage once their work had proven value. Here's the specific timeline. For works assigned after January 1, 1978, the creator can file a termination notice during a five-year window that opens 35 years after the original assignment date. That means if you sold your screenplay or underlying rights in 1990, your termination window opened in 2025 and closes in 2030. If you miss that window, the studio owns your copyright permanently under the original deal terms — regardless of what your work ultimately became worth. The reason most creators don't exercise termination rights isn't ignorance — it's the cost of legal counsel, the technical complexity of the filing requirements, and the fact that studios have institutional resources to contest or delay reclamation. The most well-documented real example: Sylvester Stallone successfully reclaimed ownership of Rocky from MGM through copyright termination. Rocky was released in 1976. Stallone had sold the rights as part of the original production deal. Decades later, he filed the required termination notices under federal law, reclaimed his ownership stake, and now controls how the Rocky character and franchise is developed going forward. For any filmmaker or writer who signed away IP rights in the 1980s or early 1990s — your termination window is either open right now or opening soon. If you haven't spoken to a copyright attorney about whether you have standing to reclaim, this is the conversation to have now.
