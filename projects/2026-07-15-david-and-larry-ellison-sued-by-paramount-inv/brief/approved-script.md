A Paramount shareholder filed suit against David Ellison and Larry Ellison this week, alleging they made an illegal side deal with the Trump administration to lock down regulatory approval for their merger. Strip away the political noise and look at the legal theory here, because it reveals something real about how major media deals are getting done right now. The allegation is essentially that antitrust approval — a process supposed to run on competitive merits — was traded as a political favor. That's a serious claim. But even before discovery, it surfaces something every major media acquirer already understands: regulatory approval for studio consolidations has become a political variable, not just a legal one. Studios aren't just filing antitrust paperwork and waiting for a ruling. They're managing Washington relationships as a core part of M&A strategy. Larry Ellison is Oracle — one of the most politically connected technology companies in the country. His son David is taking over Paramount. The shareholder's allegation is that those two realities got bundled into a single negotiation, and that the people whose capital was at stake — the Paramount shareholders — weren't party to it. This lawsuit probably doesn't stop the merger. But it puts the structure of how big media deals actually get approved under legal scrutiny and on public record. That's worth understanding for anyone trying to read what Hollywood consolidation really looks like when it's happening.
